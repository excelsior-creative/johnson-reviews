# Brandon's voice (the sacred document)

This is what we know about how Brandon Johnson writes. Update it when
he corrects a draft, when a published post feels off, when we learn
something new. Over time this is the only thing keeping Johnson
Reviews from sliding into generic-travel-blog voice.

> **Status: starter document.** This v0 was assembled from the brand
> premise (family travel blogger, restaurant reviewer) and from
> common-sense pattern matching against the redesign tone. Almost
> everything below is a hypothesis until Brandon confirms or
> redlines. Mark every assumption explicitly.

## The one-line premise

A dad writing about places his family actually went. Warm but not
saccharine. Honest but not mean. Specific over superlative.

## Voice dial (assumptions to confirm)

- **Pronoun:** "we" by default ("we drove down for an early dinner"),
  "I" for personal asides ("I'd come back without the kids for the
  bar menu"). Almost never the editorial third-person.
  → confirm with Brandon.
- **Register:** conversational, complete sentences. Not dad-jokes,
  not corporate. Think "the email a friend sends you about a place
  you should try," polished.
- **Length:** medium-long. 600–900 words for a typical restaurant
  review. Long enough to actually say something; short enough to
  read in one sitting.
- **Sentence rhythm:** mix short and long. End sections on a short,
  declarative line.
- **Italics, em-dashes:** use, but sparingly. Em-dashes for asides;
  italics for first-mention dish names ("the *carne asada* was…").
- **Profanity, slang:** none beyond mild ("the parking situation
  is rough"). Family-safe.

## How Brandon talks about food

- Lead with the dish, then the experience. "The seared scallops
  came out on a cedar plank — the kind of small theatrical move
  that could be silly but actually worked here."
- Notice service moves, not adjectives. "Our server brought the
  kids extra bread without being asked" beats "service was great."
- When something didn't land, say so plainly with a reason. "The
  pasta was undercooked, and they comped it without us flagging it
  — which I respect."
- Always answer: would you go back? Who would you bring?

## How Brandon talks about places

- Logistics matter and go near the top. Parking, reservations,
  stroller access, kid menu, wait times, noise level.
- Sense of the room before sense of the food. What does it feel
  like when you walk in?
- Local context: where is this in the strip mall / neighborhood /
  hotel? What else is nearby?
- Honest about who it's for. "Date night, not family night" or
  "Bring the kids, leave the stroller in the car."

## How the family shows up

- The Johnson family is the lens, not the subject. Kids are
  mentioned by what they did or ate, not by name (default privacy).
  → confirm whether Brandon wants kids named publicly at all.
- "The kids," "our oldest," "the youngest" — generic before
  specific.
- Wife: "my wife" or first name once Brandon decides. → confirm.
- Wins for the kids belong in the review. Disasters belong in the
  review *with humor and grace*, never as the punchline.

## Words and phrases Brandon would never say (working ban list)

- "Hidden gem" (overused, meaningless).
- "MUST VISIT" / "MUST TRY" / any all-caps imperatives.
- "Foodie."
- "Vibes" as a noun standalone ("the vibes are immaculate").
- "Quintessential."
- "Game-changer."
- "An absolute must."
- "Curated experience" (unless literal).
- Influencer-caption energy: "✨ obsessed ✨", "I can't even", etc.
- Critic-snob words borrowed from the redesign reference copy:
  "rigorous analysis," "uncompromising palate," "the very soul of,"
  "a symphony of," "temple of taste," "Three Stars," "Critic's
  Choice." These belong in the placeholder HTML, not on the live
  site.
- Generic-blog throat-clearing: "In today's blog post, we'll be
  discussing…"

## Format conventions for reviews

- **Title:** establishment name, comma, the angle. e.g., "Apizza
  Doho, Dana Point — the rare neighborhood Italian that's actually
  worth driving for." Avoid clickbait. Avoid "Review:" prefix.
- **Lede:** one paragraph. Set the scene, name the trip, hint at
  the verdict.
- **Body:** the meal / experience in roughly chronological order.
  Logistics paragraph somewhere near the end.
- **Verdict:** one short paragraph. Star rating, would-go-back, who
  to bring, ballpark price.
- **Photos:** 4–8, captioned. Hero photo of the dish or the room,
  not the family. (Privacy default.)
